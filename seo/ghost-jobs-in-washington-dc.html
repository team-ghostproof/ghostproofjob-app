What a ghost job actually is

A ghost job is a posting that's advertised but not actively being filled. The role may already be filled internally, be paused indefinitely, or exist mainly to collect résumés. It looks identical to a genuine opening from the outside — which is exactly the problem. You write the cover letter, tailor the résumé, hit apply, and the posting simply outlives your application.

Signs a Washington posting might be a ghost job

  • It keeps getting reposted
    The same role reappears every few weeks, or has sat open for months with no close date.
  • No salary range
    A company that can't say what the role pays often hasn't finalised — or funded — the role.
  • Vague scope
    No named team, no manager, no concrete responsibilities. Real roles have real edges.
  • The company isn't otherwise hiring
    One evergreen listing and no other activity is a pipeline-building signal.
  • The listing never closes
    Genuine roles get filled and come down. "Always open" is a flag.

No single sign proves a ghost job. Together, they raise the risk — which is exactly what GhostProofJob scores for you.

What to do instead

Protect your time. Before you invest an hour tailoring an application for a Washington role, check how long the posting has been live, whether the salary is stated, and whether other hunters have flagged the company. Then spend your energy on the roles that are actually being filled.

Questions

What is a ghost job?
A ghost job is a posting that is advertised but not actively being filled — the role may already be filled, indefinitely on hold, or collected to build a résumé pipeline. It looks like a real opening, so applicants spend real time on it and usually never hear back.
How can I spot a ghost job in Washington, DC?
Watch for a posting that keeps getting reposted or has been open for months, a vague description with no named team or scope, no salary range, a company that is otherwise not hiring, and an "evergreen" listing that never closes. None of these alone proves a ghost job — together they raise the risk.
Why do employers post ghost jobs?
Common reasons include keeping a pipeline of résumés on file, signalling growth, testing a market before funding a role, satisfying an internal posting requirement when an internal candidate is already chosen, or simply forgetting to take a filled role down.
